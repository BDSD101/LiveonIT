Scoring Methodology & Framework

An analytical approach to measuring neighbourhood walkability and service accessibility in Metropolitan Melbourne.

I. Overview

The 20-Minute Neighbourhood Explorer utilises a weighted multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) to evaluate the accessibility and liveability of any given address. Each location is assigned a cumulative score out of 10.0, representing the degree to which daily requirements can be met within a local return walk.

The framework is grounded in the Victorian Government's Plan Melbourne 2017–2050 strategy, prioritising proximity, variety, and safety.

II. Core Components & Weighting

Table 1.0: Distribution of Scoring Weights

Component Weighting

Closest Services

The primary metric measuring walking distance to the nearest instance of each selected service, weighted by how often you'd use it.

  • Each service gets a distance factor: 1.0 at ≤5 min walk, decaying linearly to 0 at 20 min
  • Services beyond 1,500m generate a negative penalty proportional to how far past the threshold
  • Services not found anywhere receive the maximum penalty of −1
  • All factors are frequency-weighted, normalised and mapped to a 0–10 scale, where 5 = services sitting right at the 1,500m threshold

Service Clustering

Measures how tightly your daily and weekly services are grouped relative to each other — not relative to home.

  • Only high and medium frequency services are included (supermarkets, cafés, parks, gyms, and similar) as rarely used services should not skew the result
  • Every combination of available instances is tested to find the shortest inter-service route, with a 1.3× circuity factor applied to convert crow-flies distances to realistic walking distances
  • A missing service adds a 5,000m penalty per gap
  • Mean distance per inter-service edge is scored against a 3,000m threshold
  • 0m mean → score 10. At or beyond 3,000m → score 0

A tightly clustered group of services scores well here even if that cluster is a 15-minute walk from home. This component rewards service layout, not proximity to you specifically. Note that this score uses the closest services to each other, not necessarily the closest service to your house shown on the map page.

 5.0%

Choice & Variety

Rewards areas with multiple service providers, fostering competition and redundancy.

  • Every instance of every selected service type within 1,500m is counted and frequency-weighted
  • Compared against a benchmark of 3 instances of every selected service type within threshold
  • Score = (your total weighted options ÷ reference weighted options) × 10, capped at 10

If groceries are on sale at one particular supermarket chain, you should have diversity of services available to take advantage.

 5.0%

Housing Affordability

Applies a modest boost to more affordable suburbs to reflect genuine accessibility for diverse socio-economic groups.

  • Sourced from the Victorian Property Sales Report – Median House Price by Suburb, published quarterly by the Victorian Government's Department of Environment, Land, Water and Planning (DELWP) and available via discover.data.vic.gov.au.
  • Data is resolved at suburb level, so all addresses within a suburb share the same housing affordability score.
  • This component is designed to reflect the general affordability of living in that area, not the specific price of the property being evaluated.
  • Median house price is used as a proxy for affordability, recognising that it does not capture the full complexity of housing costs but provides a consistent comparative metric across suburbs.
  • Higher price → lower score (inverted and normalised to 0–10)
  • If no data is available this component is excluded and remaining weights scale up proportionally

Public Safety Index

Adjusts the final score based on localised crime data to reflect perceived and actual safety during pedestrian transit.

  • Sourced from Criminal Incidents by Local Government Area (LGA)
  • Published quarterly by Crime Statistics Agency Victoria and available via discover.data.vic.gov.au: Criminal Incident by LGA.
  • Total crime incidents per per 100,000 residents is used as a proxy for Public Safety, measured at LGA level.
  • Lower crime → higher score (normalised to 0–10)
  • If no data is available this component is excluded and remaining weights scale up proportionally

Service Frequency Categorisation

Not all services carry equal weight within the "Closest Services" component. Our algorithm categorises services by their typical usage frequency to ensure that essential daily needs influence the score more significantly than occasional requirements.

III. Operational Parameters

Subjective Service Selection

Unlike static walkability indices, this framework is dynamic. The score is calculated strictly based on the services selected by the user. If an individual only requires access to a library and a train station, the final rating will reflect only those specific accessibility needs, rather than a generic average of all possible amenities.

The "20-Minute" Standard

The definition of "walkable" is derived from the Victorian Government standard: a 20-minute return walk. This equates to an approximately 800-metre radius (10 minutes each way) at an average pedestrian pace. Services within 400 metres (5-minute walk) receive a full accessibility score, decaying gradually beyond that point. A 1,500m scoring threshold is applied — wider than the strict 800m boundary — so suburbs with services just beyond 800m are penalised gradually rather than scoring zero.

Data Limitations & Interpretability

While this model provides a consistent quantitative baseline for comparison, it is not an exhaustive measure of neighbourhood quality. Factors such as pavement quality, topographical changes, and shade cover are not currently accounted for in the primary scoring engine.

Strategic Policy Context

The 20-minute neighbourhood concept is a cornerstone of the Plan Melbourne 2017–2050 strategy. It aims to create vibrant, local communities where residents can meet most of their daily needs locally, fostering better health, stronger social connections, and reduced environmental impact.
